📘 ITIL 4 Foundation — Study Summary

Module 1: Key Concepts of Service Management  ·  Module 3: The Guiding Principles

Core Definitions
🏢
Service Management
Definition: A set of specialized organizational capabilities for enabling value to customers in the form of services.

It is about how organizations create, deliver and improve services.

💡
Value
Definition: The perceived benefits, usefulness, and importance of something.

Value is always co-created — it is never delivered by the provider alone.

⚙️
Service
Definition: A means of enabling value co-creation by facilitating outcomes that customers want to achieve, without the customer having to manage specific costs and risks.
🏗️
Organization
Definition: Any person or group with its own functions, responsibilities, authorities, and relationships that operate in an integrated way to facilitate value creation and fulfil common objectives.
Stakeholders
👥
Types of Stakeholders
Service Providers Service Consumers Other Stakeholders
🎭
Service Consumer Roles
  • Customers — define requirements and take responsibility for outcomes
  • Users — who use services
  • Sponsors — who authorize the budget for service consumption
✏️
Trainer Note — Consumer Roles
The Sponsor role authorizes the budget and is therefore accountable for the cost of service consumption. One person can hold multiple roles simultaneously (e.g., someone can be both user and customer).
Service Offerings
📦
Goods

Transferred from provider to consumer — the consumer takes responsibility for future use.

Example: a laptop, a physical device.

🔑
Access to Resources

Granted or licensed to a consumer under agreed terms and conditions.

Example: mobile network access, cloud storage.

🛠️
Service Actions

Performed to address a consumer's need. Carried out by the service provider per agreement.

Example: user support, training.

Service Relationships
📤
Service Provision

Activities performed by an organization to provide services.

📥
Service Consumption

Activities performed by an organization to consume services.

🤝
Service Relationship Management
Joint activities performed by a service provider and a service consumer to ensure continual value co-creation, based on agreed and available service offerings.

Output vs Outcome
📄
Output

A tangible or intangible deliverable of an activity.

Example: a report, a deployed application.

🎯
Outcome

A result for a stakeholder, enabled by one or more outputs.

Example: faster business operations enabled by the deployed app.

Utility & Warranty
⚖️
Both are needed to deliver value
🟢 Utility
Fit for Purpose
Functionality offered by a product or service to meet a particular need — "what the service does"
🔵 Warranty
Fit for Use
Assurance that a product or service will meet agreed requirements — "how the service performs"
🎢 Theme Park Example: A park with exciting rides (utility ✓) that frequently break down (warranty ✗) fails to deliver value. Similarly, rides always running (warranty ✓) but not exciting enough (utility ✗) also fails. Both must be met.
✏️
Trainer Note — Utility & Warranty
Ask yourself: "How is the service delivered?" — that is warranty. A service can do exactly what is needed (utility) but still fail if it is unreliable, insecure, or unavailable when needed (warranty).
What is a Guiding Principle?
🧭
Guiding Principle — Definition
Definition: A recommendation that guides an organization in all circumstances, regardless of changes in its goals, strategies, or type of work. A guiding principle is universal and enduring.
All activities conducted by the organization should link back, directly or indirectly, to value for itself, its customers, and other stakeholders.
The 7 Guiding Principles
1
Focus on Value

Everything the organization does should map, directly or indirectly, to value for stakeholders.

  • Know how service consumers use each service
  • Encourage a focus on value among all staff
  • Focus on value during normal operational activity, not only during improvement initiatives
  • Include focus on value in every step of any improvement initiative
2
Start Where You Are

Do not start from scratch without considering what is already available.

"Do not start from scratch and build something new without considering what is already available to be leveraged." There is likely a great deal in current services, processes, programmes, projects, and people that can be used.
  • Look at what exists as objectively as possible, using the customer or desired outcome as the starting point
  • When successful practices are found, determine if and how these can be replicated or expanded
  • Apply your risk management skills
  • Recognize that sometimes nothing from the current state can be reused
3
Progress Iteratively with Feedback

Do not attempt to do everything at once. Work in smaller, manageable sections.

  • Fast does not mean incomplete
  • Avoid analysis paralysis — "comprehend the whole but do something"
  • Minimum viable product — deliver value quickly, then improve
  • The ecosystem is constantly changing, so feedback is essential
4
Collaborate and Promote Visibility

Work together across boundaries, share information, and make work visible.

"When initiatives involve the right people in the correct roles, efforts benefit from better buy-in, greater relevance, and increased likelihood of long-term success."
  • Collaboration does not mean consensus
  • Decisions can only be made using visible data
  • Communication should happen in a way that the audience can hear
  • Identify bottlenecks, excess capacity, and understand the flow of work in progress
5
Think and Work Holistically

No service, practice, process, department, or supplier works in a vacuum.

"The outputs that the organization delivers will suffer, unless it works in an integrated way to handle its activities as a whole, rather than as separate parts."
  • Recognize the complexity of the systems
  • Collaboration is key to thinking and working holistically
  • Where possible, look for patterns in the needs of and interactions between system elements
  • Automation can facilitate working holistically
6
Keep It Simple and Practical

Use the minimum number of steps to accomplish an objective.

  • Ensure every activity contributes to the creation of value. If it doesn't — eliminate it.
  • Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication
  • Do fewer things but do them better
  • Respect the time of the people involved
  • Easier to understand, more likely to adopt
  • Simplicity is the best route to achieving quick wins
7
Optimize and Automate

Maximize the value of work carried out by human and technical resources.

Technology can help expand and undertake frequent/repetitive tasks, allowing human resources for more complex decision-making. However, automation should not always be relied upon without human intervention capability.

  • Understand and agree the context of the proposed optimization
  • Assess the current state of the proposed optimization
  • Agree what the future state and priorities should be, focusing on simplification and value
  • Ensure appropriate stakeholder engagement and commitment
  • Execute the improvements iteratively
  • Continually monitor the impact of optimization
  • Define your metrics
✏️
Trainer Note — Optimize & Automate
Use measurement to enable, not replace, analysis of the observed. Over-reliance on analytics and reporting injects bias and risk into decision-making.
