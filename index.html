Module 1 — Key Concepts of Service Management

1.1 Value & Key Definitions

Value: The PERCEIVED BENEFITS, USEFULNESS, AND IMPORTANCE of something. Value is subjective — subject to stakeholder perception.
Service Management: A SET OF SPECIALIZED ORGANIZATIONAL CAPABILITIES for enabling value to customers in the form of services.
Organization: A person or group of people with its own FUNCTIONS, RESPONSIBILITIES, AUTHORITIES, AND RELATIONSHIPS to achieve its objectives.
Service: A means of enabling VALUE CO-CREATION by facilitating OUTCOMES that customers want to achieve, WITHOUT the customer having to manage specific COSTS AND RISKS.
Key Point
Value is CO-CREATED through active collaboration. Old ITIL v3 view: provider delivers → consumer receives. New ITIL 4 view: mutual, interactive co-creation.

1.2 Stakeholders & Service Consumer Roles

Stakeholders fall into three categories: Service Providers, Service Consumers, Other Stakeholders.

RoleDefinition
Service ProviderCreates and delivers services. Can be internal (IT dept) or external (third-party).
CustomerDefines REQUIREMENTS and takes RESPONSIBILITY for outcomes of service consumption.
UserA person who USES services day-to-day.
SponsorA person who AUTHORIZES BUDGET for service consumption.

1.3 Service Offerings (GAS)

Product: A CONFIGURATION of an organization's resources designed to offer value. Typically complex; not fully visible to consumer.
Service Offering: A formal description of one or more services for a TARGET CONSUMER GROUP. Contains three components:
ComponentWho owns it?Example
G — GoodsConsumer takes OWNERSHIPMobile phone, physical server
A — Access to ResourcesRemains under PROVIDER'S controlCloud storage, mobile network access
S — Service ActionsPerformed BY the providerUser support, equipment replacement

1.4 Creating Value with Services

Output: A TANGIBLE OR INTANGIBLE deliverable of an activity — what is produced.
Outcome: A RESULT for a stakeholder, enabled by one or more outputs — the result achieved.
Relationship TypeDescription
Service ProvisionActivities performed TO PROVIDE services (manage resources, ensure access, fulfill service actions, SLM, CI)
Service ConsumptionActivities performed TO CONSUME services (manage consumer resources, perform service actions, receive goods)
Service Relationship ManagementJOINT activities by both provider & consumer to ensure continual value co-creation

1.5 Costs, Risks, Utility & Warranty

ConceptMeaningMemory hook
UtilityFunctionality offered — what the service DOES. Must support performance or remove constraints.Fit for PURPOSE
WarrantyAssurance it meets agreed requirements — HOW it performs (availability, capacity, security, continuity).Fit for USE
Key Point
BOTH utility AND warranty are required for a service to deliver value. Neither alone is enough.

Two types of Cost (consumer perspective)

  • Costs REMOVED by the service — part of value proposition (e.g., staff/technology consumer doesn't need)
  • Costs IMPOSED by the service — cost of consumption (price, training, procurement)

Two types of Risk (consumer perspective)

  • Risks REMOVED — e.g., hardware failure, staff unavailability
  • Risks IMPOSED — e.g., provider ceasing to trade, security breach
Module 2 — Key Concepts of ITIL 4

2.1 The Four Dimensions of Service Management OIPV

The four dimensions DO NOT have sharp boundaries and may overlap. External PESTLE factors (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) constrain all four.

DimensionCoversKey Points
1. Organizations & People Structure, culture, roles, competencies Leaders must champion values; trust & transparency essential; avoid silos; need for agility
2. Information & Technology Information needed + technologies used AI, cloud, blockchain; GDPR compliance; information: available, reliable, accurate, timely
3. Partners & Suppliers Relationships with other organizations, contracts Factors: strategic focus, culture, cost, expertise, regulations. SIAM coordinates multiple suppliers.
4. Value Streams & Processes How parts of org work together to enable value Value streams identify waste; processes define input→output sequences
SIAM: Service Integration and Management — using a specially established integrator to ensure service relationships are properly coordinated.

2.2 The ITIL Service Value System (SVS)

Input → SVS → Output of VALUE
Inputs: Opportunity (options to add value) + Demand (need for products/services)

#SVS ComponentDescription
1Guiding PrinciplesRecommendations guiding the org in ALL circumstances
2GovernanceDirected and controlled via: Evaluate → Direct → Monitor
3Service Value ChainInterconnected activities to deliver valuable products/services
4PracticesOrganizational resources designed for performing work
5Continual ImprovementRecurring activity at ALL levels to meet stakeholder expectations
Warn
Organizational Silos — prevent access to info, reduce efficiency, increase cost and risk. The SVS architecture discourages siloed working.

2.3 The Service Value Chain (SVC) PIEDOD

The SVC is the central element of the SVS. 6 activities that lead to value creation:

ActivityPurpose
PlanEnsure shared understanding of vision, current status & improvement direction across all 4 dimensions
ImproveEnsure continual improvement of products, services & practices across all activities
EngageUnderstand stakeholder needs; maintain transparency and good relationships
Design & TransitionEnsure products/services meet stakeholder expectations for quality, cost & time to market
Obtain/BuildEnsure service components available when needed and meet agreed specifications
Deliver & SupportEnsure services are delivered & supported according to agreed specs
Value Streams
Specific combinations of activities for a particular scenario. Not all 6 must be included. An activity can be used multiple times in one stream.

2.4 Governance

The governing body (board, executives) is accountable at the highest level for performance and compliance.

1. Evaluate — strategy & portfolios 2. Direct — assign responsibility & implement strategy 3. Monitor — performance vs policies
Module 3 — The Seven Guiding Principles
Guiding Principle: A recommendation that guides an organization in ALL CIRCUMSTANCES, regardless of changes in goals, strategies, or structure. Universal and enduring.

1. Focus on Value

All activities should link, directly or indirectly, to value for customers and stakeholders.

  • Know WHO the consumer is (customer, user, sponsor)
  • Understand WHAT is valuable to them (expected outcomes, CX/UX)
  • Collect feedback continuously; encourage value focus in ALL staff
CX (Customer Experience): Entirety of interactions a customer has — objective and subjective.
UX (User Experience): How people interact with a service and the experience they receive.

2. Start Where You Are

"Do not throw the baby out with the bathwater." — Leverage what already exists.

  • Measure and observe directly — don't rely solely on reports
  • Get data from the source; have someone with no prior knowledge observe
  • Recognize that sometimes nothing can be reused — but this is RARE
Warn — Goodhart's Law
"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." People are creative in meeting metrics.

3. Progress Iteratively with Feedback

"How do you eat an elephant? One bite at a time." — Organize work into smaller, manageable sections.

  • Seek feedback before, throughout, and after each iteration
  • Greater flexibility; faster response; detect failure earlier; better quality
  • MVP — Maximum validated learning with minimum effort
Watch Out
Analysis Paralysis: Too much analysis, nothing gets done. Understand the big picture but also make progress.

4. Collaborate and Promote Visibility

"Inclusion is always better than exclusion." — Right people in correct roles = better buy-in and relevance.

  • Cooperation > isolated/silo work; creative solutions from unexpected sources
  • Poor visibility → poor decision-making → hampers improvement
  • Identify bottlenecks, excess capacity, and uncover waste
  • Collaboration does NOT mean consensus — don't try to please everyone
  • Communicate in a way the audience can hear

5. Think and Work Holistically

"Gain a systemic understanding." — No service, practice, or department stands alone.

  • Alteration of one element can impact others — identify, analyze, plan
  • Look for patterns in needs and interactions
  • Automation can facilitate holistic working and end-to-end visibility
  • Systems Thinking: keep the larger whole in view

6. Keep It Simple and Practical

"Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication." — Minimum steps to accomplish an objective. Eliminate anything that fails to provide value.

  • Do NOT solve every exception — design rules for general exceptions
  • Do fewer things but do them better
  • Easier to understand = more likely to adopt
  • Simplicity is the best route to quick wins

7. Optimize and Automate

Maximize value of human and technical resources. Technology handles repetitive tasks; humans focus on complex decision-making.

Rule
Optimize first, then automate. Automating a suboptimal process is unlikely to achieve the desired outcome.

Path to Optimization (6 steps)

  • 1. Understand & agree the context (vision & objectives)
  • 2. Assess the current state
  • 3. Agree on future state & priorities (simplification & value)
  • 4. Ensure stakeholder engagement & commitment
  • 5. Execute improvements iteratively with metrics & feedback
  • 6. Continually monitor the impact

Awareness-Level Practices (8) — Purpose Statements

General Management
1. Information Security Management
Protect information needed to conduct business. Manage risks to CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) + Authentication & Non-repudiation. Balance: Prevention + Detection + Correction.
General Management
2. Relationship Management
Establish and nurture links between organization and stakeholders at strategic and tactical levels. Includes identification, analysis, monitoring & continual improvement of relationships.
General Management
3. Supplier Management
Ensure suppliers and their performances are managed appropriately to support seamless provision of quality products and services.
Service Management
4. IT Asset Management
Plan and manage the full lifecycle of all IT assets — maximize value, control costs, manage risks, meet regulatory requirements.
IT Asset = financially valuable component contributing to IT service delivery.
Service Management
5. Monitoring & Event Management
Systematically observe services and service components; record and report selected changes of state (Events).
Event = any change of state significant for management of a service or CI.
Service Management
6. Release Management
Make new and changed services and features available for use. A release may include infrastructure, apps, documentation, training, updated processes.
Release = a version of a service/CI made available for use.
Service Management
7. Service Configuration Management
Ensure accurate and reliable information about the configuration of services and the CIs that support them is available when and where needed.

Key terms: CI (Configuration Item), CMDB (Configuration Management Database), Configuration Record, Configuration Baseline.

Works closely with: Change Enablement, Incident Mgmt, Problem Mgmt, IT Asset Mgmt, Release Mgmt. CMDB must be kept up to date.
Technical Management
8. Deployment Management
Move new or changed hardware, software, docs, processes, or any component to live environments (also test/staging).

Types: Phased · Continuous Delivery · Big Bang · Pull

Note: Deploy to an environment; Release into the hands of users. These are separate.

Full-Depth Practices (7)

1
Incident Management — Service Management
▶
Purpose: To MINIMIZE THE NEGATIVE IMPACT of incidents by RESTORING NORMAL SERVICE OPERATION as quickly as possible.
Incident: An UNPLANNED INTERRUPTION to a service, or a REDUCTION IN QUALITY of a service.

Key Points

  • Every incident must be logged and managed
  • Prioritized based on agreed classification (highest business impact first)
  • Separate processes for: major incidents and security incidents
  • SWARMING: Many stakeholders work together initially until best person is clear

Escalation Tiers

TierResolution Path
Tier 0Self-help — users resolve themselves (Shift Left)
Tier 1Service desk resolution
Tier 2Support team escalation (by category)
Tier 3Suppliers / partners
MajorTemporary collaborative team; possible disaster recovery
SVC:EngageDeliver & Support
2
Service Request Management — Service Management
▶
Purpose: To support the AGREED QUALITY of a service by handling all PREDEFINED, USER-INITIATED service requests in an effective and user-friendly manner.
Service Request: A PREDEFINED, AGREED request for standard service — NOT a failure or incident.
  • Normal part of service delivery; usually involve standard changes
  • Steps should be well-known and proven with agreed fulfilment times
  • Standardize and automate to the greatest degree possible
  • "Shift left" towards self-service for often-repeated requests
  • Policies for redirecting misrouted requests (incidents/changes)
SVC:All except Plan
3
Service Desk — Service Management
▶
Purpose: To capture demand for incident resolution and service requests. The ENTRY POINT AND SINGLE POINT OF CONTACT for the service provider with all its users.
  • "Face of the service provider" — may also be called help desk, tech support
  • Issues must be: Acknowledged → Classified → Owned → Actioned
  • Value through empathetic, informed link between provider and users

Channels & Types

ChannelsTypes
Phone · Portal/Mobile app · Chat/Chatbots · Email · Walk-in · Text/Social media Centralized — physical single location
Virtual — agents in multiple locations (often cloud-based)

Key Skills

Incident analysis & prioritization Effective communication Empathy Emotional intelligence Customer service
SVC:All except Plan
4
Problem Management — Service Management
▶
Purpose: To REDUCE THE LIKELIHOOD AND IMPACT of incidents by identifying actual and potential causes and managing WORKAROUNDS and KNOWN ERRORS.
TermDefinition
ProblemA CAUSE, or potential cause, of one or more incidents
Known ErrorA problem that has been ANALYSED but NOT resolved
WorkaroundA solution that REDUCES/ELIMINATES impact without a full resolution

Three Phases

PhaseKey Activities
1. Problem IdentificationTrend analysis, detect duplicate incidents, RCA for major incidents
2. Problem ControlProblem analysis, document workarounds, record known errors, prioritize by risk
3. Error ControlManage known errors, identify permanent solutions, reassess regularly, evaluate workarounds each time used
Key Distinction
Problem management identifies causes & manages workarounds. Implementation of resolution is often outside its scope — initiated via Change Enablement.
SVC:ImproveDeliver & Support
5
Continual Improvement — General Management
▶
Purpose: To ALIGN the organization's practices and services with CHANGING BUSINESS NEEDS via ongoing improvement of products, services, and practices.

Exists at strategic, tactical, and operational levels. Tracked in the Continual Improvement Register (CIR).

The Continual Improvement Model — 7 Steps

StepQuestionKey Action / Risk if Skipped
1What is the vision?Define high-level vision; ensure stakeholder roles understood. Skipped → misaligned improvements
2Where are we now?Current state assessment — establishes baseline. Skipped → no baseline to measure against
3Where do we want to be?Gap analysis; set SMART objectives, CSFs & KPIs. Goal: improvement, not perfection. Skipped → unclear target, no stakeholder support
4How do we get there?Create plan; design experiments; work iteratively. Skipped → improvement flounders
5Take actionImplement; waterfall or Agile; remain open to change. Creates new current state
6Did we get there?Validate success; check relevance; iterate if needed. Skipped → no confirmation; lessons lost
7How do we keep momentum?Market successes; embed changes; prevent reversion. Skipped → progress lost
Note
Model does NOT need to be followed linearly — you may return to a previous step. Logic and common sense prevail.

SMART: Specific · Measurable · Achievable · Relevant · Time-bound

SVC:All activities
6
Service Level Management — Service Management
▶
Purpose: To set clear BUSINESS-BASED TARGETS for service levels and ensure delivery is properly ASSESSED, MONITORED, AND MANAGED against these targets.
TermDefinition
Service LevelOne or more metrics that define expected or achieved service quality
SLADocumented agreement between provider and customer on services required and expected service levels
Watermelon SLA Effect
SLAs appear green on reports but the customer is red inside (unhappy). E.g., 99.6% availability met but the 0.4% downtime hit at the worst possible moment. Solution: Metrics must truly reflect the customer's actual experience.

SLAs Must Be

  • Related to defined business outcomes — not just operational metrics
  • Agreed with customers through collaboration — not imposed
  • Understandable to all parties (customer-friendly language)
  • Reflect wider service performance, not just individual components

Two Metric Types

TypeDescriptionUsed by
OperationalAvailability %, response times, fix timesIT service providers internally
Business% successful transactions, terminal availability during business hoursAgreed based on what customer values
SVC:PlanEngage
7
Change Enablement — Service Management  (formerly Change Control)
▶
Purpose: To MAXIMIZE SUCCESSFUL CHANGES by ensuring risks are properly assessed, authorizing changes, and managing the CHANGE SCHEDULE.
Key Stat
80% of incidents in many organizations are caused by unauthorized change.

Three Types of Change

TypeDescriptionAuthorization
Standard Low risk, pre-authorized, well-understood, fully documented. Often automated. Pre-authorized when procedure is created — not repeated each time.
Normal Scheduled, assessed, and authorized following a process. Can range from low to very major. Initiated by a Change Request (RFC). Change models define roles.
Emergency Implemented ASAP (resolve incident / security patch). Some documentation may be deferred. Separate Change Authority (small number of senior managers). Expedited process — still requires testing & assessment.
Important Distinction
Change Enablement = changes to products and services.
Organizational Change Management = the people aspects of change. These are DIFFERENT.
SVC:All activities
